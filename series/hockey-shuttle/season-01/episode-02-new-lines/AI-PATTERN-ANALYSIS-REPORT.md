## SECTION 4: SPECIFIC REVISION EXAMPLES

### Example 1: Physical Reactions

**CURRENT (AI-flagging):**
> "Sophia's chest tightened. Her heart was doing that arhythmic thing again. Her hands were shaking."

**HUMANIZED OPTIONS:**

**Option A (Visceral):**
> "Sophia couldn't breathe right. Everything felt too tight—her ribs, her throat, the space between her heart and her lungs."

**Option B (Unexpected detail):**
> "Sophia's vision went sharp at the edges, the way it did before she served in a tournament. Fight or flight. Her body didn't care that this was just a conversation."

**Option C (Strategic vagueness):**
> "Sophia felt it—that thing that happened when you stood too close to the edge of something. Not fear, exactly. More like physics."

**Technique:** Vary the vocabulary, use unexpected comparisons, sometimes leave the feeling unnamed.

---

### Example 2: Stop-Start Dialogue

**CURRENT (AI-flagging):**
> "I want to—" He stopped. Started again. "God, Sophie, it's been the best part of—" He stopped. "I don't know how to ask this."

**HUMANIZED OPTIONS:**

**Option A (More natural interruption):**
> "I want to—" He laughed, frustrated with himself. "God, Sophie, you're like—the best part of moving back here?" It came out as a question. "That's not—I'm screwing this up."

**Option B (Body language instead):**
> "I want to—" He shoved his hands in his pockets, pulled them out, ran one through his hair. "Sophie, you make me feel like myself again. Before everything got so..." The gesture said what he couldn't.

**Option C (Interrupt differently):**
> "I want—" He cut himself off with a noise of frustration. "How do I even say this? You've been back three weeks and I can't stop thinking about—about MIT again. About who I used to be."

**Technique:** Vary how characters interrupt themselves (laughter, gestures, sound, redirection). Not always stop-start-stop.

---

### Example 3: Three-Item Emotional Lists

**CURRENT (AI-flagging):**
> "Terrified? Thrilled? Guilty? All of it at once?"

**HUMANIZED OPTIONS:**

**Option A (Messier):**
> "She felt like she'd won something and stolen it at the same time. Or maybe she hadn't won anything. Maybe this was just guilt with a pulse."

**Option B (Single dominant emotion):**
> "Guilt, mostly. But guilt that felt suspiciously like hope around the edges."

**Option C (More specific):**
> "She felt the way she did after winning that semifinal—exhausted, shaky, unsure if she'd earned it or just gotten lucky."

**Technique:** Either one dominant emotion with qualification, or unexpected comparison. Avoid the A-B-C list structure.

---

### Example 4: Heart/Chest Repetition

**CURRENT (Used 7+ times):**
> "Her chest tightened."

**HUMANIZED ALTERNATIVES (pick different ones each time):**

1. "Her ribcage felt two sizes too small."
2. "Something squeezed under her sternum—hope or panic or both."
3. "Breathing got complicated."
4. "She felt it in her teeth, somehow. That electric almost-pain."
5. "Her whole torso went tense, like her body was trying to protect something."
6. "It hit her somewhere behind her collarbone—that oh shit feeling."
7. "Her lungs forgot how to work for a second."

**Technique:** Never use the same physical description twice. Force yourself to find new ways to express emotional embodiment.

---

### Example 5: Transition Word Variety

**CURRENT (Overused pattern):**
> "Then he walked away, and Sophia stood there trying to understand. Then her phone buzzed. Then Maya appeared."

**HUMANIZED OPTIONS:**

**Option A (Vary structure):**
> "He walked away. Sophia stood there trying to understand—or maybe not understand, just process. Her phone buzzed. When she looked up, Maya was there."

**Option B (Cut transitions entirely):**
> "He walked away. Sophia stood there trying to understand. Her phone buzzed. Maya, somehow: 'You okay?'"

**Option C (Different transitions):**
> "He walked away. For a moment Sophia just stood there. Her phone buzzed—Maya, checking in."

**Technique:** "Then" is useful but overused. Try: for a moment, when, somehow, after a beat, cutting entirely.

---

### Example 6: "Complicated" Overuse

**CURRENT (Used 15+ times):**
> "This was complicated." / "It's complicated." / "complicated feelings"

**HUMANIZED ALTERNATIVES:**

1. "This was a mess." / "This was chaos with good intentions."
2. "She couldn't explain it, even to herself."
3. "There were too many moving parts—his parents, Becca, her own fear."
4. "It felt impossible and inevitable in the same breath."
5. "Simple would have been easier. This was everything but simple."
6. "The situation had too many sharp edges."
7. "Nothing about this was straightforward."

**Technique:** "Complicated" is AI's favorite word for relationship ambiguity. Ban it and force more specific descriptions.

---

### Example 7: Arhythmic Word Choice

**CURRENT (Teen narrator using medical term):**
> "Her heart was doing something arhythmic."

**HUMANIZED OPTIONS:**

**Option A (Natural teen voice):**
> "Her heart couldn't decide on a rhythm—too fast, then a weird pause, then hammering to catch up."

**Option B (Comparison):**
> "Her heart was doing that thing from gym class when you run too fast too soon—stuttering, uneven, kind of painful."

**Option C (Simpler):**
> "Her heart was all over the place."

**Technique:** Technical terms feel AI-generated. Teens use comparisons or simpler language.

---

### Example 8: Sentence Structure Breaking

**CURRENT (Mechanical pattern):**
> "She was definitely lying to herself. But sometimes lying felt safer than the truth."

**HUMANIZED OPTIONS:**

**Option A (Different connector):**
> "She was definitely lying to herself—had been for days, really—because lying felt safer than the truth."

**Option B (Fragment):**
> "She was definitely lying to herself. Safer that way. The truth felt too big to hold."

**Option C (Longer thought):**
> "She was definitely lying to herself, but sometimes lying was the only thing that made it possible to get through lunch without crying in the bathroom."

**Technique:** Not everything needs "But" as a connector. Use fragments, em dashes, or longer thoughts.

---

### Example 9: Dialogue Interruption Variety

**CURRENT (Repeated pattern):**
> "Sophia—"
> "I'm fine."

**HUMANIZED OPTIONS:**

**Option A (Let it trail):**
> "Sophia..." Maya's voice did that thing where the end curled up, not quite a question.
> "I'm fine."

**Option B (Different energy):**
> "Sophie." Just her name, said like it contained a whole argument.
> "Don't. I'm fine."

**Option C (Physical instead):**
> Maya opened her mouth—
> "I'm fine," Sophia cut her off.

**Technique:** Vary how characters interrupt/redirect. Tone, physicality, different words, trailing off.

### Example 10: Variable/Equation Metaphor Reduction

**CURRENT (Used 7+ times):**
> "She was the variable that didn't fit the equation."

**KEEP this once or twice (it's good for Sophia's character), but REDUCE frequency:**

**When you use it (Chapter 1, establishing her math mind):**

**Technique:** Strong metaphor established once or twice. After that, vary the language.
