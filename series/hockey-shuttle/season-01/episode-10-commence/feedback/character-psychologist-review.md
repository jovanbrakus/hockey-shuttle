# Character Psychology Review: Episode 10 - "Commence"

**Reviewer:** Character Psychologist
**Episode:** Season 1, Episode 10 - "Commence"
**Date:** November 9, 2025
**Focus:** Character authenticity, teen voice, psychological realism, emotional dynamics

## Executive Summary

Episode 10 demonstrates strong character development and generally authentic teen voices. The season finale successfully shows the protagonists' growth while maintaining psychological realism. However, there are areas where emotional processing feels rushed, certain character reactions seem too mature for their age, and some relationship dynamics could be more realistically complex.

**Overall Character Authenticity Rating: 7.5/10**

---

## Strengths

### 1. Authentic Decision-Making Anxiety
The portrayal of Ethan's NHL decision process is psychologically accurate for a 17-year-old facing life-altering choices:
- **Chapter 2, lines 91-94:** His oscillation between confidence and panic is spot-on
- **Chapter 2, lines 422-424:** "I'm walking away from $2.5 million dollars" / "You're walking toward something better" - This exchange captures teen catastrophizing beautifully
- The multiple revisitations of the decision (Chapter 6 with second and third offers) realistically shows how teens often need multiple attempts to solidify major choices

### 2. Parent-Teen Dynamic Evolution
Caroline's transformation and her relationship with Ethan shows realistic psychological progression:
- **Chapter 1, lines 219-224:** Caroline's announcement of filing for divorce - timing feels authentic
- **Chapter 2, lines 326-365:** The dinner conversation about her own regrets is psychologically rich
- The gradual shift from enabler to independent supporter tracks well

### 3. Realistic Teen Romance Dynamics
Some moments capture authentic teenage relationship patterns:
- **Chapter 3, lines 283-287:** The prom nervousness and physical awareness
- **Chapter 5, lines 162-168:** Ethan's separation anxiety about Toronto training
- **Chapter 6, lines 1136-1149:** MIT overwhelm threatening the relationship

---

## Areas Needing Improvement

### 1. Overly Mature Emotional Processing

**Issue:** Both protagonists, especially Sophia, display emotional intelligence beyond typical 17-year-olds.

**Examples:**
- **Chapter 1, lines 457-462:** Sophia's analysis "Is this the life you want, or the life you were taught to want?" - Too sophisticated for most teens
- **Chapter 2, lines 179-184:** Her immediate insight about fear types feels more like a therapist than a teenager

**Suggestion:** Add more emotional messiness. Teens rarely have such clear insights in real-time. Consider:
```markdown
Instead of: "Is this the life you want, or the life you were taught to want?"
Try: "But like... is this even what YOU want? Or is it just your dad talking in your head?"
```

### 2. Unrealistic Lack of Petty Conflict

**Issue:** The relationship is too healthy and mature. Real teen couples have stupid fights.

**Missing Elements:**
- No jealousy when discussing time with other people
- No miscommunications or overreactions to texts
- No small resentments building up
- Perfect communication during stress (unrealistic)

**Suggestion:** Add minor relationship friction in Chapter 5:
```markdown
After line 520 (Toronto training):
Sophia didn't mention how Priya's brother had been flirting with her at practice.
It wasn't important. But when Ethan casually mentioned his study group included
"this really smart girl from his coaching course," something tight and stupid
lodged in her chest.
```

### 3. Too-Perfect Support System Responses

**Issue:** Adults are unrealistically wise and supportive without their own complications.

**Examples:**
- **Chapter 1, lines 115-144:** Mom's job story is too perfectly relevant
- Parents always say exactly the right thing at the right time

**Suggestion:** Add parental anxiety and mistakes:
```markdown
After Chapter 1, line 369:
"I still think you're making a mistake," Dad said quietly to Mom, thinking
I couldn't hear. "He should take the money."
"It's not our choice," Mom replied, but her voice wavered.
```

## Character-Specific Analysis

### Sophia Liu

**Authenticity Rating: 7/10**

**Strengths:**
- Competitive drive feels genuine
- Badminton dedication rings true
- Problem-solving approach to relationship

**Weaknesses:**
- Too emotionally regulated for 17
- Never loses her temper
- Advice-giving feels therapist-like
- No selfish moments (unrealistic)

**Suggested Improvements:**
1. Add a moment where she gives bad advice because she's also just a teenager
2. Show her being petty or jealous at least once
3. Let her have a breakdown about her own pressures (not just supporting Ethan)

### Ethan Price

**Authenticity Rating: 8/10**

**Strengths:**
- Anxiety spirals feel very real
- Identity confusion post-emancipation is authentic
- Physical reactions to stress well-portrayed
- Regression under pressure (calling Robert "Dad" again) would be realistic

**Weaknesses:**
- Recovers from major decisions too quickly
- Doesn't lash out at Sophia when stressed (teens often misdirect anger)
- Too articulate about his emotions

**Suggested Improvements:**
1. Add speech disfluencies when stressed: "I just... I can't... like, how am I supposed to..."
2. Show him snapping at Sophia unfairly at least once
3. Include more physical manifestations of anxiety (not eating, insomnia details)

### Caroline Price

**Authenticity Rating: 8.5/10**

**Strengths:**
- Delayed rebellion feels psychologically accurate
- Mix of guilt and empowerment well-balanced
- Practical struggles of starting over at 42

**Weakness:**
- Transforms too quickly from enabler to empowered
- No backsliding or moments of weakness

### Robert Price

**Authenticity Rating: 6/10**

**Issue:** Becomes too one-dimensional as pure antagonist. Real controlling parents have moments of genuine care mixed with toxicity.

**Suggestion:** Add complexity through Caroline's memories or Ethan's conflicted feelings:
```markdown
"He used to read to me," Ethan said suddenly. "When I was little. Before hockey
consumed everything. He did voices for all the characters."
```

## Dialogue Authenticity Issues

### 1. Too Much Emotional Vocabulary

**Problem Examples:**
- "Generational money" - teens don't use this phrase
- "Emotional autonomy" - too clinical
- "Processing" - overused therapy-speak

**Better Alternatives:**
- "That's like, stupid amounts of money"
- "I need to figure my shit out without him"
- "I need to think" or "My brain is melting"

### 2. Unrealistic Conflict Resolution

**Chapter 2, lines 463-470:** The graduation eve conversation is too neat. Real teens would:
- Circle back to the same anxieties
- Not resolve things so cleanly
- Have more "I don't know" moments
- Use humor to deflect more

### 3. Missing Teen Speech Patterns

**What's Missing:**
- Filler words ("like," "basically," "literally")
- Incomplete sentences when emotional
- Topic jumping in conversations
- Inside jokes or references we don't fully understand
- More profanity when adults aren't around

---

## Emotional Arc Authenticity

### Pacing Issues

**Chapter 3 (Prom) to Chapter 4 (Graduation):** The emotional processing is too smooth. Teens don't move from milestone to milestone this cleanly.

**Suggestion:** Add emotional whiplash:
```markdown
The day after prom, Sophia woke up crying for no reason she could name. The
perfection of last night made everything feel like it was ending too fast.
```

### Missing Teenage Emotional Patterns

1. **Mood Swings:** Both characters maintain steady emotional states for too long
2. **Catastrophizing:** Not enough "this is the end of the world" moments about small things
3. **Emotional Contagion:** They don't feed off each other's anxiety enough
4. **Regression:** No one acts younger when stressed (very common in teens)

---

## Relationship Dynamic Concerns

### 1. Too Equal Power Balance

Real teen relationships have power imbalances that shift. Currently, Sophia always has the wisdom/stability role. This is unrealistic.

**Suggestion:** Let Ethan be the stable one sometimes:
```markdown
"Sophie, you're spiraling about badminton," Ethan said. "You always do this -
make everything into the biggest decision ever. It's just summer training."
```

### 2. No Codependency Red Flags

Given their trauma bonding, some unhealthy dependency would be realistic:
- Checking phones obsessively
- Anxiety when apart
- Making decisions based on what the other wants
- Identity fusion issues

### 3. Perfect Communication Under Stress

**Chapter 6, MIT arrival:** They're too good at expressing needs clearly. Real stressed teens would:
- Have at least one stupid fight about nothing
- Misinterpret neutral comments as attacks
- Project their own fears onto each other

## Recommendations for Authentic Teen Psychology

### 1. Add More Physical Reactions
- Stress acne before big decisions
- Appetite changes (not eating or stress eating)
- Sleep disruption descriptions
- Nervous habits (nail biting, hair touching)

### 2. Include Social Media Anxiety
- Completely missing: How are they presenting this on Instagram?
- College decisions are hugely performative for teens
- The pressure of public relationships

### 3. Show Identity Fragmentation
- Teens try on different personas
- They act differently with different people
- Code-switching between groups
- Uncertainty about who they "really" are

### 4. Add Peer Comparison Anxiety
- Limited to Madison subplot
- Need more "everyone else seems to have it figured out"
- FOMO about paths not taken
- Social positioning concerns

1. "Choosing yourself" - too self-actualized
2. Calmly discussing $2.5 million - would be more overwhelming
3. Perfect boundary-setting with parents

2. Making pro/con lists then ignoring them completely

4. Dramatic declarations they immediately take back
5. More "I don't know" as a complete response

## Conclusion

Episode 10 succeeds in showing character growth and maintaining narrative momentum through the season finale. The core emotional journeys are sound. However, the characters often display emotional intelligence and communication skills beyond their years. Adding more authentic teenage messiness, regression, and imperfect coping mechanisms would increase psychological realism without sacrificing the story's heart.

The relationship between Sophia and Ethan, while beautiful, needs more realistic conflict and codependency to reflect how trauma-bonded teenage couples actually function. The supporting cast needs development to feel like real teenagers rather than plot devices.

**Strongest Element:** Ethan's anxiety and decision paralysis
**Weakest Element:** Too-perfect communication and conflict resolution
**Quick Fix:** Add three "stupid fights" that real teens would have
**Long-term Development:** Build in more identity confusion and peer dynamics

The episode has a strong foundation. With these adjustments, the characters would feel not just compelling but authentically seventeen.
