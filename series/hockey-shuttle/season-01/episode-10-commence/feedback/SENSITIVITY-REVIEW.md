# SENSITIVITY REVIEW: Episode 10 "Commence"
## The Boy Who Knew Me First - Season 1 Finale

**Reviewer Role:** Sensitivity Reader
**Date:** November 9, 2025
**Episode Length:** ~30,000 words (6 chapters)
**Review Scope:** Comprehensive sensitivity analysis across all representation categories

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Overall Assessment:** Episode 10 presents a thoughtful, emotionally complex finale with generally strong, authentic character development. The episode excels in portraying healthy relationship dynamics, addressing parental control/abuse, and showing young adults navigating autonomy. However, several areas require attention regarding representation of Asian-Canadian identity, potential stereotyping, and deeper exploration of socioeconomic privilege.

**Major Strengths:**
- Healthy, respectful romantic relationship with strong communication
- Realistic portrayal of parental abuse and the emancipation process
- Female characters with agency and complex motivations
- No objectification or harmful gender dynamics
- Mature handling of consent and physical boundaries

**Areas Requiring Attention:**
- Model minority stereotype concerns with Asian protagonist
- Limited cultural identity exploration for Chinese-Canadian characters
- Socioeconomic privilege not fully examined
- Potential "loud Black woman" stereotype with secondary character
- Mental health support systems not addressed despite clear trauma

**Content Warning Recommendations:**
Readers should be advised about: emotional/psychological abuse by parent, parental control and manipulation, divorce, emancipation from family, academic pressure and anxiety, discussions of large financial decisions.

## DETAILED ANALYSIS BY CONCERN AREA

### 1. RACE AND ETHNICITY REPRESENTATION

#### **Sophia Liu - Asian-Canadian Protagonist**

**Concerns Identified:**

**A) Model Minority Stereotype**
Sophia embodies several model minority characteristics:
- Exceptional academic achievement (valedictorian, MIT acceptance)
- National champion in individual sport (badminton)
- Described as analytical, precise, methodical
- Parents are supportive, emotionally healthy, financially stable
- "She teaches like an engineer" - everything is systems and optimization

**Specific Examples:**
- Chapter 1: "You teach like an engineer. Everything's systems and optimization. 'Adjust your grip angle fifteen degrees.'" (p. 44)
- Chapter 3: Sophia as valedictorian giving inspirational speech
- Chapter 5: "I'm going to MIT for engineering. Of course I calculated." (measuring time apart in hours)

**Impact:** While Sophia is a fully developed character with agency, the cumulative effect reinforces problematic "Asians are naturally good at math/science" stereotypes.

**Recommendation:**
- Add moments where Sophia explicitly struggles academically, makes mistakes, or shows she works hard for her achievements rather than natural aptitude
- Include dialogue acknowledging the pressure of model minority expectations
- Show her making choices that go against "perfect Asian student" stereotype

**B) Cultural Identity Erasure**

**Concern:** Sophia's Chinese-Canadian identity is mentioned through her surname only. There's no exploration of:
- Cultural traditions or practices
- Language (does she speak Mandarin/Cantonese?)
- Experience of being Asian in predominantly white spaces
- Racism or microaggressions she may face
- Connection to Chinese culture or community

**Specific Gap:** In Chapter 1, Sophia's family has breakfast together - no cultural foods mentioned, no cultural markers in home description.

**Impact:** This creates a "default white" narrative where ethnicity is cosmetic rather than meaningful identity.

**Recommendations:**
- Include casual cultural details: food, language, family traditions
- Address racism/microaggressions Sophia might face (especially as Asian woman in STEM)
- Explore her relationship to her heritage, even if she's assimilated
- Consider moments where her parents speak Chinese or reference cultural values
- At MIT, address being Asian in a predominantly white institution

**C) Jay Patterson - Ambiguous Asian Identity**

**Concern:** Jay (Ethan's roommate) is described as "quiet Asian guy" without specificity.

**Quote:** "He was with his roommate—a quiet Asian guy who looked mildly overwhelmed." (Chapter 6, p. 104)

**Problem:** "Asian" is not a monolith. Is Jay Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Vietnamese, South Asian, etc.? This lumping together erases distinct cultures and identities.

**Recommendation:** Specify Jay's ethnicity or avoid racial descriptor if it's not relevant. Give him distinct cultural identity if Asian identity is important to his character.

#### **Maya Williams - Black Character**

**Concerns Identified:**

**A) "Loud Black Woman" Stereotype**

**Specific Examples:**
- "I'm Maya Williams. Your roommate... I'm very organized and kind of loud. If that's going to drive you crazy, tell me now." (Chapter 6, p. 56)
- "Maya was wonderful and exhausting in equal measure." (Chapter 6, p. 258)
- "Maya immediately took charge, getting everyone to share names, majors, and 'one weird fact about yourself.'" (Chapter 6, p. 240)

**Analysis:** While Maya is portrayed positively (smart, athletic, going to MIT), describing a Black woman as "loud" plays into harmful stereotypes about Black women being "too much," aggressive, or lacking refinement.

**Counter-Evidence:** Maya is also shown as:
- Organized and competent
- Mechanicalengineering major (STEM achievement)
- Track athlete (discipline and skill)
- Supportive roommate

**Recommendation:**
- Reconsider the descriptor "loud" - use more specific language like "outgoing," "energetic," "enthusiastic," "extroverted"
- Show Maya in quiet, contemplative moments too
- Ensure she has depth beyond being Sophia's supportive friend
- Give her own challenges, vulnerabilities, and character arc

**B) Limited Development**

**Concern:** Maya appears primarily as supportive roommate character. In Episode 10, she doesn't have significant independent plot or depth.

**Recommendation:** In future episodes, give Maya:
- Her own challenges and storylines
- Complex relationships beyond being Sophia's friend
- Moments of vulnerability and struggle
- Agency in her own narrative

### 2. GENDER REPRESENTATION

**Strengths Identified:**

**A) Sophia Liu - Strong Female Protagonist**
- Has clear goals independent of romantic relationship
- Makes own decisions about career and education
- Not defined by relationship to male protagonist
- Physically strong (athlete) without being masculinized
- Intellectually capable without being "nerdy girl" trope
- "I choose MIT for a lot of reasons. You're one of them... Not the only reason. But a really good one." - healthy relationship dynamic

**B) Caroline Price - Complex Character Arc**
- Starts as complicit in abuse but takes accountability
- "I should have protected you better" shows self-awareness
- Her arc from controlled wife to independent woman is earned
- Gets job, files for divorce, builds new life - agency restored
- Not "saved" by son but inspired to save herself

**C) Madison Hayes - Mature Ex-Girlfriend**
- No catty ex-girlfriend drama
- Gracious at prom, moves on to healthy relationship
- "You two make sense in a way we never did" - mature acknowledgment
- Avoids "jealous ex" trope

**D) Sophia's Mother - Supportive Without Being Controlling**
- Shares her own story of choosing career vs. relationship
- Supports Sophia's choices without imposing
- Emotionally present without being overbearing
- Models healthy parenting

**Concerns Identified:**

**A) Caroline's Fast Recovery**

**Issue:** Caroline goes from 17-year abusive marriage to job, apartment, and independence very quickly. This might:
- Underestimate trauma recovery time
- Suggest leaving abuse is easier than it is
- Not show realistic challenges of starting over at 42

**Specific Example:** Gets job after one interview despite 17-year employment gap (Chapter 3, p. 453)

**Recommendation:**
- Acknowledge difficulty of transition more explicitly
- Show Caroline in therapy or support groups
- Address financial challenges more realistically
- Don't make recovery look too easy

**B) Women as Emotional Labor**

**Minor Concern:** Women characters (Sophia, Caroline, Maya, Sophia's mom) do significant emotional work supporting men's decisions.

**Example:** Sophia spends considerable time helping Ethan process his NHL decisions, though this is somewhat balanced by him supporting her badminton choices.

**Recommendation:** Ensure emotional labor is reciprocal and acknowledged.

### 3. MENTAL HEALTH REPRESENTATION

**Concerns Identified:**

**A) Trauma Without Treatment**

**Issues:**
- Ethan shows clear signs of anxiety, PTSD from parental abuse
- "I'm terrified. Completely." repeated throughout
- No mention of therapy, counseling, or professional mental health support
- Characters expected to "figure it out" alone

**Specific Examples:**
- Ethan's panic attacks before big decisions (Chapter 2, Chapter 6)
- Sleep issues: "I couldn't sleep. At 7 AM, Ethan texted" (Chapter 6)
- Constant self-doubt and imposter syndrome
- Caroline's trauma from 17-year abusive marriage

**Impact:** This normalizes dealing with trauma without professional help, suggesting resilience alone is sufficient.

**Recommendations:**
- Add mention of therapy/counseling as option
- Show characters seeking mental health support as strength
- Have MIT provide counseling resources (realistic for college setting)
- Acknowledge trauma recovery is ongoing process requiring help

**B) Anxiety Normalization**

**Analysis:** While showing anxiety realistically is good, there's no framework for healthy coping mechanisms beyond "pushing through."

**Examples:**
- Constant catastrophizing without intervention
- Physical symptoms (can't sleep, might throw up) treated as normal
- Academic stress building without healthy outlets

**Recommendations:**
- Show healthy coping strategies (exercise, meditation, therapy)
- Have characters recognize when stress is unhealthy
- Normalize seeking help before crisis point

**C) Positive Representation**

**Strengths:**
- Anxiety is not romanticized
- Characters acknowledge fear and uncertainty honestly
- Mental health struggles don't make characters "weak"
- Support from friends/family shown as important

### 4. PARENTAL ABUSE AND CONTROL

**Strengths:**

**A) Realistic Portrayal of Psychological Abuse**
- Robert's manipulation tactics are authentic
- Control through money, expectations, guilt
- Gaslighting: "you're making emotional decisions"
- Using Caroline to manipulate Ethan
- Undermining confidence: "not as smart as you think you are"

**B) No Victim Blaming**
- Story never suggests Ethan "deserves" abuse
- Caroline's complicity is acknowledged but contextualized
- Emancipation portrayed as necessary, not overreaction

**C) Recovery Is Shown as Process**
- Ethan still struggles with Robert's influence
- Legal freedom doesn't immediately equal emotional freedom
- "Legal freedom doesn't mean he'll stop trying" - realistic

**Concerns:**

**A) Robert as One-Dimensional Villain**

**Issue:** Robert is purely antagonistic with no nuance or humanity shown.

**Impact:** While his behavior is clearly abusive, lack of complexity makes him less realistic. Real abusers often have moments of charm, genuine care, or complexity that make abuse harder to recognize.

**Recommendation:**
- Consider one scene showing Robert's perspective (not to excuse, but to understand)
- Show why Caroline stayed 17 years (was he always like this? Did he change?)
- Could add moment where Robert genuinely believes he's helping
- This would make the abuse more realistic, not less serious

**B) Caroline's Quick Transformation**

**Previously mentioned under gender, but relevant here:**
- 17 years of complicity to clarity and action in weeks
- May underestimate difficulty of leaving abusive relationship
- Could inadvertently suggest other victims "just need to be brave"

**C) Class Privilege in Escaping Abuse**

**Issue:** Ethan can afford lawyer, emancipation process, MIT tuition. Caroline gets job and apartment easily.

**Impact:** Not all abuse victims have these resources. Story might suggest escaping abuse is easier than it is.

**Recommendation:**
- Acknowledge the privilege of having resources
- Show financial challenges more realistically
- Don't suggest their path is available to all abuse victims

### 5. SOCIOECONOMIC CLASS AND PRIVILEGE

**Major Concerns:**

**A) Turning Down Generational Wealth**

**Issue:** Ethan turns down $2.5M-$3.5M and this is treated as purely personal choice without examining class privilege.

**Specific Examples:**
- "Walking away from $2.5 million dollars versus student debt and uncertainty" (Chapter 2, p. 42)
- Most people can't afford to turn down this money
- No discussion of how rare this opportunity is
- Treated as individual choice rather than enormous privilege

**Impact:** May alienate readers who don't have luxury of choosing education over immediate financial security.

**Recommendations:**
- Explicitly acknowledge this is privileged position
- Show characters recognizing how rare this choice is
- Address that most people can't afford to prioritize "growth" over money
- Consider financial stress more realistically

**B) MIT Affordability Not Addressed**

**Concerns:**
- Both characters attend MIT but financial stress barely mentioned
- Sophia mentioned on "scholarship" once but not explored
- "Student debt" mentioned but not calculated or worried about
- No work-study, no financial aid discussions, no family sacrifice shown

**Quote:** "Unless I get scholarships" - mentioned once, never followed up

**Impact:** Makes MIT seem accessible to anyone accepted, erasing real financial barriers.

**Recommendations:**
- Show financial aid process and stress
- Address cost explicitly ($80K+ per year)
- Show characters working, applying for scholarships
- Acknowledge financial privilege if families can afford it

**C) Caroline's Easy Job Acquisition**

**Issue:** After 17 years out of workforce, Caroline gets architecture-adjacent job quickly after one interview.

**Reality:** Career gaps make job searching extremely difficult, especially for women over 40.

**Recommendation:**
- Show more struggle in job search
- Acknowledge she may be starting at lower level
- Address financial stress of divorce more realistically

### 6. RELATIONSHIP DYNAMICS AND CONSENT

**Strengths:**

**A) Healthy Communication**
- Sophia and Ethan discuss boundaries explicitly
- "Tell me what you're thinking" - open communication modeled
- Disagreements handled maturely
- Mutual support without codependency

**B) Consent and Physical Boundaries**
- First overnight: "both fully clothed, fell asleep talking" - clear boundaries
- Parents handle maturely: "You're both fully clothed, you clearly didn't sleep much"
- Physical affection is consensual and age-appropriate
- No pressure for sexual activity

**C) Independence Within Relationship**
- Both choose personal opportunities (Toronto, coaching course)
- "We both choose ourselves first. And then we choose each other anyway."
- Healthy balance of togetherness and independence
- Neither sacrifices dreams for relationship

**D) No Toxic Tropes**
- No "if you loved me, you would..."
- No jealousy or possessiveness
- No controlling behavior
- No "love triangles" or manufactured drama

**Minor Concerns:**

**A) Relationship as Stakes**

**Issue:** Ethan's fear of "losing" Sophia is repeated motivation for decisions.

**Examples:**
- "I don't want to lose you" appears multiple times
- NHL choice partially motivated by keeping relationship

**Concern:** Could suggest relationship should influence major life decisions more than personal goals.

**Mitigation:** Story does push back against this - Sophia insists he choose for himself. Generally handled well.

### 7. DISABILITY REPRESENTATION

**Finding:** No disabled characters present in Episode 10.

**Recommendation for Future:**
- Consider including disabled students at MIT (realistic - disability is common)
- If including disabled characters, ensure they have agency and aren't inspiration porn
- Show accessibility as normal (ramps, accommodations) without making it plot point

### 8. BODY DIVERSITY AND APPEARANCE

**Strengths:**

**A) Positive Body Representation**
- Sophia's "athlete shoulders" mentioned positively
- "badminton calluses visible on her hands because I refused to cover them" - authentic detail
- Prom dress: "You look like yourself, just elevated" - not about conforming
- No fat-phobia detected
- No characters shamed for appearance

**B) Athletes Not Masculinized**
- Sophia is strong, athletic, feminine all at once
- No "you're not like other girls" messaging
- Female athletes respected for skill

**Minor Observations:**

**A) All Major Characters Physically Capable**
- No body diversity in terms of size, ability
- All major characters are athletes or physically active

**B) Appearance Descriptions**
- Characters described as conventionally attractive
- No acne, braces, or "awkward" physical traits despite being 17-18
- Very little physical description diversity

**Recommendation:**
- Include more body diversity in secondary characters
- Show that MIT students come in all body types
- Don't equate achievement with conventional attractiveness

### 9. LGBTQ+ REPRESENTATION

**Finding:** No LGBTQ+ characters identified in Episode 10.

**Observation:** This is a heteronormative narrative. While not harmful, lack of representation is notable.

**Recommendation for Series:**
- Include LGBTQ+ characters at MIT (statistically realistic)
- Could include queer students in friend groups, classes
- Avoid making queerness the only character trait
- If including queer characters, ensure authentic, own-voices sensitivity review

### 10. LANGUAGE AND MICROAGGRESSIONS

**Strengths:**
- No slurs detected
- No obviously offensive language
- Respectful dialogue throughout

**Minor Concerns:**

**A) "Asian guy"**
- Jay described as "Asian guy" without specificity (Chapter 6)
- Lumps all Asian ethnicities together

**B) Gendered Language**
- Generally appropriate
- Some traditional gender roles (mother cooks, emotional labor)
- Not egregiously problematic but worth noting

**C) Ableist Language**
- "Crazy" used casually several times
- "Insane" used as synonym for unreasonable
- Consider alternatives: "wild," "intense," "overwhelming"

**Examples:**
- "You'd be crazy not to take it" (multiple instances)
- "That's insane" (money amounts)

**Recommendation:** Replace ableist casual language with specific descriptors.

## CONCLUSION

Episode 10 "Commence" is a thoughtfully written, emotionally complex finale with significant strengths in character development, relationship dynamics, and thematic depth. The portrayal of parental abuse, healthy relationships, and young adults claiming autonomy is excellent and will resonate with YA readers.

However, the episode would benefit from deeper attention to:
1. Asian-Canadian cultural identity and avoiding model minority stereotypes
2. Examination of socioeconomic privilege
3. Mental health support systems
4. Avoiding stereotypes for Black characters
5. More explicit acknowledgment of barriers that make "choice" harder for marginalized groups

**The core story is strong and valuable.** With targeted revisions addressing the concerns outlined above, this episode can maintain its emotional impact while providing more authentic, inclusive representation.

**Estimated Revision Level:** Moderate - Most concerns can be addressed through additions and language changes rather than structural rewrites.

## POSITIVE REINFORCEMENT

**What Makes This Episode Excellent:**

1. **The Core Message** - "Is this the life I want, or the life I was taught to want?" is powerful and universally resonant

2. **Relationship Modeling** - Sophia and Ethan's relationship shows healthy communication, boundaries, and mutual support that YA readers need to see

3. **Female Characters** - Sophia, Caroline, Madison, and Sophia's mother are all complex, with agency and growth

4. **Abuse Recognition** - Never excuses Robert's behavior, shows that legal freedom and emotional freedom are different, portrays recovery as process

5. **Academic Realism** - MIT stress is portrayed honestly without glorifying burnout culture

6. **Age-Appropriate Complexity** - Emotional depth suits YA readers while maintaining appropriate content

This episode has tremendous heart and important messages. With sensitivity revisions, it will be even stronger.
