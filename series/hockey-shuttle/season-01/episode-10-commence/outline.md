**Timeline:** May 16th - August 15th
**Length:** 120 pages (30,000 words)
**Structure:** 6 chapters
**Starting Point:** May 15th midnight - NHL offer received, emancipation granted, finally together

---

## EPISODE ARC

### Primary Plot
Ethan must decide between NHL entry (Detroit Red Wings, $2.5M signing bonus) or MIT commitment by June 1st deadline while navigating final high school milestones and preparing for college life with Sophia.

### Emotional Journey
From crisis and impossible choice → to clarity and self-knowledge → to celebration and closure → to anticipation and new beginning → to confident commitment → to thrilling uncertainty

### Season 1 Resolution
- Ethan achieves legal autonomy and self-determination
- Sophia wins national championship, proves herself
- Their relationship survives extreme pressure
- Both escape controlling families and choose their own paths
- Ready to begin college together at MIT

### Season 2 Setup
- College life at elite university with different pressures
- Long-term relationship challenges in new environment
- Balancing multiple commitments (academics, sports, relationship)
- New external conflict threatening their partnership

**Sophia's Valedictorian Speech (Full Text):**

"Good morning, graduates, families, teachers, and distinguished guests.

When Principal Harrison asked me to be valedictorian speaker, I almost said no. Not because I don't have things to say—anyone who knows me knows I always have things to say—but because I've only been at this school for nine months. Nine months. Most of you have known each other since kindergarten.

I moved to Winnipeg in September from a smaller town where I was the badminton girl. Here, I became the new girl, the scholarship student, the one who didn't know anyone. I expected to be lonely. Instead, I found something I didn't know I was looking for.

I found a community that celebrates excellence in all its forms. Athletes and artists, scientists and musicians, writers and builders. I found teachers who genuinely care whether we understand, not just whether we pass. I found classmates who pushed me to be better, even when I didn't think I could be.

But most importantly, I learned something about choosing yourself.

This year, many of us faced impossible choices. College or career. Safety or dreams. What others expect versus what we actually want. And here's what I learned: the only wrong choice is the one you make for someone else.

Some of us are going to Ivy League schools. Some are joining the military. Some are starting businesses, taking gap years, going to community college, starting families, or pursuing professional sports. Every single path is valid if it's genuinely yours.

I'm going to MIT in August—and yes, I'm terrified. I'm leaving behind a sport I love, a city I've grown to care about, and all the safety of what I know. But I'm choosing it. Not because it's what my parents want, though they're supportive. Not because it looks good on paper. But because when I imagine my future, this is the one that makes me excited instead of just secure.

And I learned this from watching all of you. The artist who chose Parsons over pre-med. The hockey player who chose education over professional contracts. The musician who chose Berklee over business school. The writer who chose to take a year to write instead of rushing to college.

You taught me that courage isn't about making the biggest choice or the most impressive choice. It's about making the honest choice.

So here's my challenge for all of us: In five years, ten years, twenty years—when we're living whatever lives we've built—I hope we can look back and say 'I chose this. Not my parents, not my teachers, not society's expectations. Me.'

Because that's what graduation really means. Not that we've finished something, but that we're finally free to begin.

To the class of 2025: We made it. Not just through classes and exams and social drama, but through a year that tested us in ways we didn't expect. We're stronger than we knew. Braver than we believed. And ready for whatever comes next.

Thank you, and congratulations."
